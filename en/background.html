Tsang Tsou Choi "King of Kowloon" (1950s - Early 21st Century)

Although Tsang Tsou Choi's activities spanned across Hong Kong, including Kowloon, Central, and Western Sai Ying Pun on Hong Kong Island, his graffiti traces can also be found in Sai Ying Pun, a historic neighborhood in western Hong Kong Island. He was one of Hong Kong's earliest and most representative street graffiti artists.

He began street graffiti from the late 1950s to early 1960s, continuing for over 50 years until his death in 2007. He used calligraphy brushes to write family genealogies, proclaiming himself "King of Kowloon" and claiming sovereignty over Kowloon.
  • Creative Form: Used calligraphy brushes to write family genealogies, with repeated phrases like "King of Kowloon Tsang Tsou Choi"
  • Time Span: From the late 1950s until his death in 2007, spanning over half a century
  • Social Impact: Initially viewed as vandalism, gradually accepted by some citizens, and later recognized by artists and cultural circles as "folk art"
  • Relationship with Sai Ying Pun: Although Sai Ying Pun was not Tsang Tsou Choi's core activity area (he was mainly active in Kowloon), as part of city-wide graffiti activities, he can be considered one of the indirect sources of early graffiti culture in Sai Ying Pun

The Rise of Modern Graffiti Walls
🎨
Art Revitalization Projects After the 2010s

The famous "graffiti walls" or "Instagram spots" in Sai Ying Pun today are not derived from Tsang Tsou Choi-style grassroots graffiti, but rather created by urban art revitalization projects promoted by the government or public in recent years.

Most of these graffiti are permitted art projects, fundamentally different from illegal graffiti, aiming to combine historic neighborhoods with contemporary art.
  • Main Areas: Concentrated in alleys such as Centre Street, Ki Ling Lane, and Shek Tong Tsui, forming outdoor art districts like "ARTLANE"
  • Creative Themes: Co-created by local and international artists, covering themes such as old Hong Kong style, flora and fauna, and abstract patterns
  • Starting Time: Approximately from the late 2010s to early 2020s, as part of the "Urban Renewal Project" in the "Sai Ying Pun Art Festival"
  • Official Attitude: Most of these graffiti are permitted art projects, fundamentally different from illegal graffiti, aiming to combine historic neighborhoods with contemporary art
  • Modern Art Origin: After the 2010s, legal street mural projects promoted by art groups and communities constitute the main body of Sai Ying Pun's graffiti walls today

From Grassroots Protest to Urban Art: The Dual Origins of Sai Ying Pun Graffiti

Sai Ying Pun's graffiti does not have a single origin, but rather represents a fusion of grassroots protest-style graffiti (represented by Tsang Tsou Choi) and contemporary urban art revitalization.

From Tsang Tsou Choi's use of calligraphy brushes to write family sovereignty declarations on the streets in the mid-20th century, to the government and artists collaborating to create the "ARTLANE" outdoor gallery in the second decade of the 21st century, Sai Ying Pun's street art has undergone a transformation from "illegal markings" to "cultural assets".

This dual origin gives Sai Ying Pun graffiti unique cultural depth: it carries the voices and memories of Hong Kong's grassroots people while showcasing the creative vitality of contemporary urban renewal.

Today's Sai Ying Pun graffiti walls are not only popular Instagram spots for tourists but also vivid examples of Hong Kong's old district revitalization, community participatory art, and historical cultural preservation.
